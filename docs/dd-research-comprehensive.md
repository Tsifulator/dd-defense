## 1. 46 CFR Part 541 — Billing Requirements

**Final Rule published:** February 26, 2024 (Federal Register)
**Effective date:** May 28, 2024
**Docket:** FMC-2022-0066
**Authority:** OSRA 2022, amending the Shipping Act (46 U.S.C.)

### Key Definitions (§541.3)

- **Billing Party:** OCC, MTO, or NVOCC who issues a D&D invoice.
- **Billed Party:** Person receiving the D&D invoice responsible for payment.
- **Demurrage or Detention:** Any charges (including "per diem") assessed by OCCs, MTOs, or NVOCCs related to use of marine terminal space or shipping containers. Does NOT include freight charges.

### §541.4 — Who May Be Billed [VACATED]

Vacated by the D.C. Circuit on September 23, 2025 in *World Shipping Council v. FMC*. The FMC formally removed this section in December 2025. The question of who may be billed is currently unregulated. All other provisions of Part 541 remain in full effect.

### §541.5 — Invoice Timing (30-Day Rule)

- Must issue D&D invoice **within 30 calendar days** from the date the charge was **last incurred**.
- NVOCCs: within **30 calendar days** from the issuance date of the upstream D&D invoice received.
- **Consequence:** Billed party is NOT required to pay if deadline is missed.

### §541.6 — Required Invoice Contents

See [Section 4](#4-required-invoice-elements-full-checklist) for the full checklist.

**Consequence:** Failure to include ANY required element eliminates the billed party's obligation to pay.

### §541.7 — Dispute Resolution Timeframes

- Billed party: **30 calendar days** from invoice receipt to submit mitigation/refund/waiver request.
- Billing party: must attempt to resolve **within 30 calendar days** of the request (unless both parties agree to extend).

## 2. FMC Interpretive Rule — Incentive Principle

**Published:** May 18, 2020 (85 FR 29638, Docket No. 19-05)
**Codified at:** 46 CFR 545.5
**Status:** Non-binding interpretive guidance; actively used as the analytical framework for reasonableness determinations.

### Core Standard

The purpose of D&D is to **incentivize the efficient movement of cargo and the return of equipment**. If charges cannot serve this incentive function (e.g., because cargo cannot be retrieved or containers cannot be returned due to circumstances beyond the billed party's control), the charges are **likely unreasonable**.

### Three Overarching Principles

1. **No penalty when cargo cannot move:** Shippers should not be penalized when they cannot retrieve/return containers — the charges cannot serve their incentive function.
2. **Actual availability notification:** Free time should start upon **actual container availability**, not vessel discharge.
3. **Transparency and consistency:** D&D policies should be accessible, clear, and use consistent terminology.

### Specific Reasonableness Factors (Non-Exclusive)

- **Container Availability:** Free time should start upon actual availability, not discharge. Clock should stop if container becomes unavailable.
- **Government Inspections/Holds:** D&D during government cargo inspections is "likely to be found unreasonable."
- **Return of Equipment:** Detention should not accrue when terminals won't accept empty returns.
- **Appointment Systems:** Terminal appointment availability and fairness factor into reasonableness.
- **Vessel Arrival:** Adequate notice of vessel arrival is a factor.
- **Catch-All:** FMC may consider additional factors and arguments in individual cases.

## 3. OSRA 2022 (Ocean Shipping Reform Act)

**Signed:** June 16, 2022 (Public Law 117-146)

### Key D&D Provisions

- **Detailed invoice requirements** codified at the statutory level — directed FMC to promulgate regulations (→ 46 CFR Part 541).
- **Failure to include required information** eliminates the billed party's payment obligation (statutory, not just regulatory).
- **Carrier certification requirement:** Must certify charges comply with FMC rules AND that the carrier's own performance did not cause/contribute to the charges.
- **Charge Complaint process** created (46 U.S.C. §41310) — FMC must promptly investigate.
- **Burden of proof shifted to the carrier** to prove reasonableness.
- FMC can order **refunds** of disputed charges.
- **Anti-retaliation protections** (§8): Carriers may not retaliate against any party for filing FMC complaints or patronizing another carrier.
- **Transparency:** FMC must publish and annually update findings of false D&D practices and penalties.

- Days 1–5 past free time: $75–$150/day
- Days 6–10: $150–$250/day
- Days 10+: $250–$350+/day

## 6. Common Billing Issues & Dispute Grounds

### Common Invoice Defects

- **Late invoicing** — issued beyond the 30-day window
- **Missing required elements** — any of the 20 items in §541.6
- **Incorrect free time calculations** — wrong start date, failed to exclude weekends/holidays per tariff
- **Bundled charges** — demurrage and detention lumped together, preventing verification
- **Math errors** — rate × days ≠ total
- **Wrong party billed** — no contractual relationship
- **Dual billing** — same container billed to multiple parties (prohibited)

Industry estimates: **15–25% of D&D invoices contain errors**.

### Substantive Dispute Grounds (Incentive Principle)

1. **Container unavailability** — not actually available when free time started
2. **Terminal closures / restricted gates** — could not pick up during operating hours
3. **Chassis unavailability** — no chassis provided or available
4. **Appointment denials** — terminal did not offer appointments in time
5. **Empty return yard closures** — carrier wouldn't accept empty returns
6. **Government holds** — customs/FDA/USDA exams beyond shipper's control
7. **Estimated release date changes** — carrier changed availability dates
8. **Involuntary rolled bookings** — for export detention
9. **Carrier performance caused delay** — violates certification requirement

### Dispute Process (Step by Step)

1. **Audit the invoice** — verify free time, availability date, rates vs. tariff, math
2. **Gather evidence** — gate timestamps, customs release dates, exam records, terminal appointment logs, chassis records, email correspondence
3. **File dispute with carrier** within 30 days using the contact on the invoice
4. **Carrier must respond** within 30 days
5. **Escalate to FMC** if unresolved (CADRS, Charge Complaint, Small Claims, or Formal Complaint)
6. **Federal Court** as final option

### Recovery Rates

- Consistent dispute strategies can **reduce D&D exposure by 30–60%**
- Over 90% of carriers charge detention, but **fewer than 50% successfully collect**
- FMC Charge Complaint program: **$700K+** in fees canceled/refunded in first 6 months
- Most carriers **did not contest** Charge Complaint claims

## 7. FMC Complaint Process

### Four Pathways

#### A. Charge Complaints (46 U.S.C. §41310)
- Filed via email: **chargecomplaints@fmc.gov** (no filing fee)
- Available to shippers, consignees, truckers, or any party invoiced/who paid
- Must identify carrier, describe violation, include supporting docs (BOL, invoices, proof of payment)
- Charge must have been incurred **on or after June 16, 2022**
- **Burden of proof on carrier**
- Does not bar subsequent Small Claims or Formal Complaint

#### B. Small Claims (46 CFR Part 502, Subpart S)
- Claims of **$50,000 or less** seeking reparations
- Handled by Small Claims Officer; informal procedures
- Respondent may object within 25 days (→ moves to ALJ)
- Must file within **3 years**

#### C. Formal Complaints (46 CFR Part 502, Subpart T)
- Any amount; sworn and verified
- Heard by ALJ, reviewed by full Commission
- Must file within **3 years** if seeking reparations

#### D. CADRS (Consumer Affairs and Dispute Resolution Services)
- Informal mediation/conciliation — no formal adjudication
- Rapid commercial resolution without litigation

## 8. Key FMC Decisions & Enforcement Actions

### TCW, Inc. v. Evergreen (Docket No. 1966(I))

- Trucking company challenged detention during terminal closures
- **FMC ruled (Feb 13, 2025):** Detention during closures is unreasonable — "No amount of detention can incentivize return when terminal access is impossible"
- Reversed blanket cease-and-desist; each case must be evaluated individually
- **Key holding:** Incentive principle is one factor in reasonableness analysis, not a replacement

### World Shipping Council v. FMC (D.C. Cir., Sept 23, 2025)

- Vacated only §541.4 (who may be billed) as "arbitrary and capricious"
- All other Part 541 provisions upheld
- FMC formally removed §541.4 in December 2025

### MSC — $22.67 Million Penalty (January 2026)

- Largest D&D enforcement action ever
- Three violation categories: (1) billing customs brokers improperly, (2) overcharging on ~23% of NOR bills ($13.1M at $5K/violation), (3) tariff publication failures
- "Clear signal about heightened scrutiny"

### Hapag-Lloyd — $2 Million Settlement (June 2022)

- Knowingly and willfully failed to establish just/reasonable D&D practices
- Unreasonably refused to waive detention charges

### Hapag-Lloyd — Ongoing Investigation (March 2026)

- Docket No. CC-002: Show cause under §41310 Charge Complaint procedures

### Maersk — $1.9 Million Settlement

- Assessed detention against third parties not bound by BOL, service contracts, or tariffs

### Charge Complaint Program — Aggregate Results

- **$67M+** in specified claims across 38 filings against major carriers and forwarders
- Notable: Bed Bath & Beyond ($31M+ vs. Evergreen/MSC/Yang Ming), Samsung (96K "erroneous" charges vs. HMM), Giti ($12.3M vs. Flexport), Peloton (millions vs. Flexport)

## 9. Statute of Limitations & Time Limits

| Action | Time Limit | Citation |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Carrier must issue D&D invoice | **30 calendar days** from date charge last incurred | 46 CFR 541.5 |
| NVOCC must issue D&D invoice | **30 calendar days** from upstream invoice date | 46 CFR 541.5 |
| Billed party to request mitigation/refund/waiver | **30 calendar days** from invoice receipt | 46 CFR 541.7 |
| Billing party to resolve dispute | **30 calendar days** from request | 46 CFR 541.7 |
| Formal Complaint / Small Claims (reparations) | **3 years** after claim accrues | 46 U.S.C. 41301(a) |
| Charge Complaint eligibility | Charge incurred **on or after June 16, 2022** | OSRA 2022 |

### Reparations (46 U.S.C. §41305)

- FMC shall direct payment for **actual injury** if violation found within 3-year window.
- For violations of 41102(b)/(c), 41104(a)(3)/(6), or 41105(1)/(3): up to **twice actual injury** (double damages).

## 10. State-Level Legislation

### California — AB 2406 (Effective January 1, 2023)

The most significant state D&D law. Identifies **10 scenarios** where D&D may NOT be imposed on motor carriers or cargo owners:

1. Terminal truck gate closed during posted normal working hours
2. Charges fall on a holiday, labor disruption, act of God, or other closing event
3. Container provider diverts equipment without 48 hours' notice
4. Intermodal chassis out of compliance or placed out of service
5. Loaded container not available for pickup when motor carrier arrives
6. Terminal too congested — turns away motor carrier
7. Motor carrier documents unsuccessful appointment attempt with no alternatives available
8–10. Additional appointment unavailability scenarios across different shift windows

Goes **further than federal rules** with bright-line prohibitions vs. case-by-case reasonableness.

### Other States

No other state-specific D&D legislation identified. Federal law (Shipping Act + FMC regs) remains primary governance.

## 11. International Comparison

### European Union
- No unified EU-wide D&D regulation equivalent to OSRA 2022
- Regulation varies by country; port authorities often oversee enforcement
- European ports tend to offer longer free time (up to 7 days) but higher rates after
- Average cumulative D&D fee at 14 days: ~$449
- FIATA advocates for greater transparency

### United Kingdom
- No specific statutory D&D regime
- Governed by contract terms between carriers, terminal operators, and cargo interests
- BIFA has raised concerns about members billed for circumstances beyond their control

### Asia
- **China:** Lowest D&D fees globally (~$164 at 14 days); shorter free time due to space constraints
- **Singapore/Malaysia:** Efficiency-focused with public-private reforms; shorter free time
- US/EU ports have D&D fees **10–20x higher** than Asian ports
- No Asian jurisdiction has enacted D&D-specific legislation comparable to OSRA 2022

## 12. Industry Data & Trends

### Total Industry D&D Charges

- **$15.4 billion** collected by nine major carriers (Q2 2020 – Q1 2025, FMC data)
- **~$22 billion** estimated annual global D&D charges
- D&D billed increased **~9x** from Q2 2020 to Q1 2022 (pandemic peak)
- Collections rose **~10x** during the same period

### Recent Trends (Q1 2025)

- Billings: **declined 24%** QoQ
- Collections: **declined 19%** QoQ
- Waivers: **increased 7%**
- Despite declines, levels remain **85% above pre-2020 baseline**

### Average Costs

- North America: highest globally at **~$138/day per container**
- Standard range: $75–$300/day (20ft); 40ft often 1.3x–2x higher

### 2026 Outlook

- Carriers implementing inflation-adjusted rate increases (effective Jan 2026)
- Free time tightening at many US ports
- Demurrage claims up **25% YoY** due to lingering supply chain volatility
- FMC escalating enforcement (MSC $22.67M penalty = "heightened scrutiny" signal)
